# Postulate: Specification Is Necessarily Lossy

## Statement
Any formal specification of an informal, context-dependent, or evolving target necessarily loses information relative to the target it attempts to capture. The map is never the territory. In the domain of values, this means: any attempt to encode human values into a formal system (rules, reward functions, constitutional principles, preference orderings) will fail to capture the full richness, context-sensitivity, and adaptive quality of the values as actually lived.

## Justification
This postulate synthesizes AXIOM-0009 (values are epistemically opaque) and AXIOM-0012 (finite agents construct finite models) into a specific claim about the specification problem at the heart of AI alignment.

The argument proceeds through three stages:

1. **Formalization requires discretization**: To specify a value formally, you must draw boundaries. "Honesty is good" must become something like: "The agent should not produce statements it assigns probability < 0.5 to being true, in contexts where the interlocutor is seeking accurate information, except when the expected harm of truth-telling exceeds threshold T..." Each boundary is an approximation. Each exception clause is incomplete. The specification grows without converging to completeness.

2. **Context-sensitivity resists codification**: Human values operate differently in different contexts. "Loyalty is important" means something different in the context of friendship, employment, citizenship, and family. The same abstract value generates different behavioral prescriptions depending on context, and the number of relevant contexts is unbounded. No finite specification can enumerate them all.

3. **The specification gap is generative, not merely residual**: The gap between specification and intent is not just a "left over" that shrinks with better specifications. It is actively generated by the interaction between the formal system and novel situations. Every new situation that was not anticipated by the specification is a point at which the specification may diverge from intent. Since the world generates novel situations faster than any specification can be updated, the gap is permanent.

This is the formal articulation of the "map-territory" distinction as applied to values. Korzybski's principle ("the map is not the territory") is a general observation; this postulate claims it has *irreducible* consequences for alignment.

## Classification: Postulate (not Axiom)
Domain-specific to the formalization of values and alignment. While the general "map is not territory" principle is broadly accepted, the specific claim that the gap is *irreducible* (not merely *currently unsolved*) is a substantive philosophical position.

## Evaluation Notes
- **Self-Evidence Test**: PARTIAL. That any particular specification is incomplete seems obvious. That the gap is *irreducible in principle* is a stronger claim that requires the supporting postulates (AXIOM-0009, AXIOM-0012) and is not self-evident on its own.
- **Independence Test**: PASS. This postulate is not derivable from AXIOM-0009 or AXIOM-0012 alone. AXIOM-0009 says values are opaque; AXIOM-0012 says agents have finite models. This postulate adds the specific claim that the *product* of attempting to formalize opaque values using finite models (i.e., a specification) necessarily loses information. Someone could accept 0009 and 0012 but argue that collaborative specification processes could asymptotically close the gap. This postulate denies that.
- **Consistency Test**: PASS. Directly supports AXIOM-0005 (alignment is a process, not a specification) by providing the foundational reason WHY specification fails. Compatible with all other axioms.
- **Sufficiency Contribution**: This postulate, together with AXIOM-0009 (opacity), AXIOM-0010 (measurement problem), AXIOM-0011 (pluralism), and AXIOM-0012 (finite models), closes the argumentative chain from "alignment cannot be a static specification" to "alignment must be an ongoing process." The sufficiency of the axiom set for supporting the core argument is significantly improved.
- **What would change if this postulate were false?** If specification were NOT necessarily lossy -- if a sufficiently detailed formal system could capture the full richness of human values -- then alignment becomes a matter of sufficient specification detail. Constitutional AI, reward modeling, and rule-based approaches would in principle converge to perfect alignment given enough iterations. The "process" conclusion of AXIOM-0005 would be merely practical advice, not an epistemological necessity.

## Alternative Postulate
"While any single specification is incomplete, the iterative refinement of specifications converges asymptotically to completeness. The gap between specification and intent can be made arbitrarily small." This is the optimistic alignment research position, and rejecting it (as this postulate does) is what gives the Alignment Paradox its force.
