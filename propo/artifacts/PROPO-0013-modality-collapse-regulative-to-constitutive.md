# The Modality Collapse Proposition

## Statement
A regulative alignment process (PROPO-0011) that succeeds in producing stable, broadly accepted value outcomes is structurally vulnerable to modality collapse: a degenerative transition in which participants begin treating the process's outputs as constitutive truths (fixed specifications of correct values) rather than provisional results of ongoing deliberation, thereby reactivating the four impossibility barriers (THEORICA-0001) that the regulative modality was designed to avoid. Success breeds confidence, confidence breeds rigidity, and rigidity converts regulative ideals into constitutive dogma.

## Logical Form
Let RA be regulative alignment, CA be constitutive alignment, Success(RA) be sustained successful operation, Conf be participant confidence in outputs, and B be the four impossibility barriers.

(1) Success(RA) --> increases Conf in RA's outputs [success produces confidence]
(2) High(Conf) --> participants treat outputs as fixed rather than provisional [confidence breeds rigidity]
(3) Outputs treated as fixed = de facto CA [reification of process outputs into specifications]
(4) B blocks CA [from THEORICA-0001]
(5) Therefore: Success(RA) --> risk of collapse into CA --> risk of reactivating B

The proposition asserts a structural tendency, not an inevitability:
For all RA: Success(RA) --> IncreasedRisk(Collapse(RA, CA)) unless active counter-measures prevent reification

## Categorization
- **Analytic/Synthetic**: synthetic -- the claim that successful regulative processes tend to collapse into constitutive rigidity is a substantive empirical and sociological claim about institutional dynamics, not derivable from definitions alone.
- **Necessary/Contingent**: contingent -- it is conceivable that a regulative process could be designed with sufficient anti-reification mechanisms to prevent modality collapse indefinitely. The proposition claims this is a structural risk, not that it is inevitable.

## Justification
SYNTHESIS-0001 identified the Modality Collapse Risk as one of three new contradictions generated by the Alignment Modality Thesis. The risk follows from a well-documented pattern in institutional dynamics: successful processes tend to become institutionalized, and institutionalization tends to convert flexible practices into rigid rules.

Historical analogues support this pattern:
- The scientific method was designed as a regulative process (ongoing inquiry with self-correction), but in practice, scientific consensus often functions as constitutive authority (challenging established paradigms is punished, not rewarded).
- Democratic constitutions were designed as regulative frameworks (enabling ongoing governance), but specific constitutional interpretations often become constitutive dogma (treated as the final word rather than as living interpretation).
- Religious traditions that began as regulative practices (ongoing spiritual inquiry) often calcified into constitutive doctrines (fixed creeds and orthodoxies).

The mechanism of collapse involves epistemic humility (LOGOS-0011) erosion. When a regulative process produces good outcomes consistently, participants have less experiential basis for tentativeness. The very success of the process undermines the epistemic humility that the process requires to remain regulative.

This creates a paradox of success: the better regulative alignment works, the greater the risk that it will stop working -- because success undermines the epistemic conditions that enable the regulative modality. The alignment process must actively resist its own success by maintaining tentativeness about its outputs even when those outputs appear to be working.

## Evaluation Notes
**Strength**: This proposition identifies a genuine structural vulnerability of the regulative approach. It is not merely a "what if" but a prediction grounded in well-documented institutional dynamics and the internal logic of the modality distinction.

**Potential counter-argument**: The protocol architecture (THEORICA-0002) could include explicit anti-collapse mechanisms in Layer 1 (the unamendable core) or Layer 2 (the amendable protocol): sunset clauses requiring periodic re-examination of all outputs, mandatory adversarial review, or structural randomization that prevents any output from being treated as settled. If such mechanisms are effective, modality collapse is preventable.

**Deeper concern**: Anti-collapse mechanisms are themselves subject to collapse. If the mechanisms are effective, participants may become confident in the mechanisms rather than in the outputs -- which is a meta-level modality collapse (confidence that the anti-collapse system works may make the system less vigilant). This suggests an infinite regress of anti-collapse measures, which may itself be impractical.

**Negation**: "Regulative alignment processes are NOT structurally vulnerable to modality collapse -- their success does NOT tend to convert them into constitutive systems." This would require showing that regulative processes have built-in resistance to reification that does not erode with success.
