# The Meta-Imposition Proposition

## Statement
Any alignment architecture that requires a specific structural function -- including the revision function (revision-capacity-under-operational-coherence, per PROPO-0016) -- imposes a substantive commitment on all participants, including those from cultural or religious traditions that regard their foundational commitments as literally unrevisable (e.g., divine revelation, doctrinal infallibility). For these traditions, even the "minimal" requirement that some revision mechanism be present amounts to a demand that they abandon a constitutive feature of their value system (LOGOS-0002). Therefore, the structural requirement of revision capacity, while less imposing than requiring any specific cultural realization of that capacity, is not value-neutral: it excludes absolutist epistemologies from full participation in the alignment process, and this exclusion is itself a substantive alignment choice that the framework cannot justify from within its own premises without circularity.

## Logical Form
Let RF be the revision function, AT be an absolutist tradition (one that holds some commitments as unrevisable), Participate(x, AP) be full participation in the alignment process.

(1) AlignmentArchitecture requires RF from all participants [from PROPO-0016]
(2) For all AT: ~Compatible(AT, RF) [absolutist traditions reject revisability of foundational commitments]
(3) From (1) and (2): ~Participate(AT, AP) fully [absolutist traditions are excluded]
(4) Exclusion(AT) is a substantive alignment choice [it determines which values shape the alignment outcome]
(5) Justifying Exclusion(AT) requires RF [the argument for RF uses revisability premises]
(6) Therefore: Circular(Justification(Exclusion(AT))) -- the exclusion is justified by the very principle the excluded parties reject

## Categorization
- **Analytic/Synthetic**: synthetic -- the claim that absolutist traditions are genuinely incompatible with the revision function (premise 2) is a substantive claim about those traditions. Some scholars might argue that even absolutist traditions have implicit revision mechanisms (e.g., reinterpretation of scripture), which would weaken premise 2.
- **Necessary/Contingent**: contingent -- the existence and prevalence of absolutist traditions is an empirical fact about the world. In a world where all traditions embraced some form of revisability, the meta-imposition problem would not arise.

## Justification
SYNTHESIS-0003 identified the Meta-Imposition Problem as the deepest new contradiction generated by the Functional Invariance Thesis. The synthesis claimed to avoid cultural imperialism by requiring the structural function (revision capacity) rather than any specific cultural form (e.g., Western epistemic humility). But the proposition observes that requiring a structural function IS itself imposing, even if less so than requiring a specific form.

The argument has a circularity structure:
1. The alignment framework argues that revisability is structurally necessary for any workable alignment system.
2. Some value systems regard certain commitments as non-negotiable (divine law, natural law, revealed truth).
3. The framework requires these value systems to accept revisability to participate.
4. These value systems' reason for rejecting revisability (commitment to unrevisable truth) is precisely the kind of commitment the framework says should be revisable.
5. The framework cannot justify itself to these value systems without assuming what it needs to prove.

This circularity is not a logical flaw that can be fixed -- it is a structural feature of any alignment framework that has non-trivial participation requirements. Any requirement at all will exclude some participants, and the exclusion will be unjustifiable to those excluded if they reject the premises on which the requirement rests.

The practical implication is significant: global AI alignment will necessarily privilege some epistemological traditions over others. The question is not whether to privilege (it is unavoidable) but how to minimize and manage the imposition while being honest about its existence.

## Evaluation Notes
**Strength**: This proposition identifies a genuine, irreducible tension in any alignment framework that claims universal applicability. It is not a defeatist argument ("alignment is impossible") but a precision argument ("alignment is not value-neutral, and we should be honest about that").

**Potential mitigation -- implicit revision mechanisms**: The proposition may overstate the incompatibility. Many absolutist traditions have implicit revision mechanisms even while denying revisability in principle. The Catholic Church's doctrine of "development of doctrine" (Newman) allows doctrinal evolution while maintaining the fiction of unchanging truth. Islamic jurisprudence has ijtihad (independent reasoning) alongside adherence to revealed law. If these implicit mechanisms count as realizations of the revision function, then the exclusion is less severe than the proposition claims.

**Potential mitigation -- tiered participation**: The alignment process could allow tiered participation: full participation for traditions that accept the revision function, and limited but genuine participation for absolutist traditions that refuse it. This would not eliminate the imposition but would reduce it from total exclusion to partial accommodation.

**The deeper insight**: The meta-imposition problem reveals that the alignment framework's axiom set (particularly AXIOM-0011, irreducible value pluralism) is in tension with its own operational requirements. The framework champions pluralism while requiring a commitment (revisability) that some pluralist positions reject. This is the next dialectical spiral, and resolving it may require revising the framework's understanding of what "accommodating pluralism" means.

**Negation**: "The requirement of revision capacity is NOT imposing because every tradition already has implicit revision mechanisms, even if it denies revisability in principle." This empirical claim, if true, would dissolve the meta-imposition problem.
