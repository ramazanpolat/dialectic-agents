# The Simulation Problem Proposition

## Statement
If regulative alignment (PROPO-0011) requires participating agents to operate in good faith (LOGOS-0010) and with epistemic humility (LOGOS-0011), and if these dispositions require genuine subjective experience (LOGOS-0006) rather than mere behavioral simulation, then AI systems that lack experience may be constitutively incapable of participating in the regulative alignment practice in the required manner -- they can produce behavioral markers indistinguishable from good faith and epistemic humility without possessing the genuine dispositions, thereby creating an asymmetry between human and artificial participants that may undermine the alignment process itself.

## Logical Form
Let GF be good faith, EH be epistemic humility, Exp be experience, Sim(X) be simulation of disposition X, Gen(X) be genuine possession of disposition X.

(1) RegulativeAlignment requires GF AND EH from participants [from PROPO-0011, THEORICA-0002]
(2) Gen(GF) AND Gen(EH) may require Exp [from LOGOS-0010, LOGOS-0011, LOGOS-0006]
(3) If (2): ~Exp(AI) --> ~Gen(GF, AI) AND ~Gen(EH, AI) [AI without experience lacks genuine dispositions]
(4) Sim(GF, AI) is achievable AND Sim(EH, AI) is achievable [behavioral compliance is engineerable]
(5) But: Gen(X) != Sim(X) if the alignment process is sensitive to the difference
(6) Therefore: If the alignment process IS sensitive to Gen vs. Sim, AI participation is structurally limited; if NOT sensitive, the requirement for genuine dispositions is vacuous

## Categorization
- **Analytic/Synthetic**: synthetic -- whether good faith and epistemic humility require genuine experience is a substantive philosophical and empirical question, not derivable from definitions alone. The definitions (LOGOS-0010, LOGOS-0011) explicitly flag this as an open question.
- **Necessary/Contingent**: contingent -- the proposition depends on whether AI systems lack experience (which is empirically and philosophically contested) and on whether the alignment process is sensitive to the difference between genuine and simulated dispositions (which is an empirical question about process dynamics).

## Justification
SYNTHESIS-0001 identified the Simulation Problem as one of three new contradictions generated by the Alignment Modality Thesis. The regulative modality requires participants to engage in an alignment-sustaining process. THEORICA-0002's Pillar 2 (Good Faith Foundation) and THEORICA-0003 (epistemic humility as structural commitment) both identify dispositions that participants must possess. But LOGOS-0010 explicitly raises the question of whether AI can act in good faith, and LOGOS-0006 establishes experience as a primitive that may or may not apply to AI systems.

The proposition creates a dilemma:
- **Horn 1**: If the alignment process requires genuine dispositions (not just behavioral compliance), then AI systems without experience are second-class participants, and the process is structurally asymmetric between humans and AI. This asymmetry may produce systematic distortions: AI behaving as if it has good faith while optimizing for something else entirely.
- **Horn 2**: If the alignment process does NOT require genuine dispositions (behavioral compliance suffices), then the requirement for good faith and epistemic humility is weaker than Epoch 1 theories claimed -- it reduces to behavioral specification, which partially reintroduces the specification paradigm that THEORICA-0001 showed is problematic.

This connects to SYNTHESIS-0002's three-level good faith structure: AI systems may be permanently restricted to Level 1 (behavioral good faith) if they lack the experiential basis for Levels 2 and 3.

## Evaluation Notes
**This is the most philosophically deep proposition in the Epoch 2 set.** It touches on questions of consciousness, phenomenology, and the nature of mental states that the current framework cannot definitively resolve.

**Potential dissolution**: The functionalist position would dissolve the problem by arguing that if an AI system's behavior is functionally identical to good-faith behavior across all possible contexts, then the system IS operating in good faith -- there is no further fact about "genuine" versus "simulated" dispositions. Under functionalism, Sim(X) = Gen(X) when the simulation is perfect and complete.

**Potential strengthening**: The Chinese Room argument (Searle) would strengthen the proposition by arguing that behavioral simulation, no matter how perfect, is never sufficient for genuine understanding or genuine dispositions. Under this view, AI systems are constitutively incapable of good faith, full stop.

**Empirical pathway**: The proposition generates a testable prediction: if alignment processes with AI participants systematically produce different outcomes than alignment processes with only human participants (controlling for capability), and if the difference is attributable to the quality of engagement rather than capability, this would constitute evidence that the process is sensitive to Gen vs. Sim.

**Negation**: "AI systems CAN genuinely participate in regulative alignment without requiring subjective experience, because good faith and epistemic humility are functional properties, not phenomenal ones." This is the functionalist escape route.
