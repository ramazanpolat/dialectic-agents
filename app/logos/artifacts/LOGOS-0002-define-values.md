# Definition: Values

## Statement
**Values** (noun, plural): Principles or standards that an agent uses — consciously or unconsciously — to evaluate outcomes, rank preferences, and guide decisions.

## Justification
Values are not just stated preferences. They include unconscious biases, evolutionary drives, cultural programming, and emergent heuristics. The definition must encompass all of these to be useful for the alignment problem.

## Genus and Differentia
- **Genus**: Principles or standards guiding evaluation and decision
- **Differentia**: Distinguished from rules by their generality, from goals by their persistence across contexts, from preferences by their normative weight (values say what SHOULD be preferred)

## Positive Example
"Human life has inherent worth" functions as a value — it guides decisions across contexts and carries normative weight.

## Negative Example
"I prefer chocolate over vanilla" is a preference, not a value. It carries no normative weight and does not guide decisions beyond the specific context.

## Dependencies
None (foundational definition)

## Evaluation Notes
Critical open question: Can values be fully articulated, or are they partly tacit (known only through behavior, not through introspection)? If partly tacit, then alignment-by-specification is fundamentally limited.
