# Classification of PROPO-0005: The Meta-Value Stability Proposition

## Statement
Epistemic humility (LOGOS-0011) is more stable across time, cultures, and value systems than any object-level value (LOGOS-0002), and therefore constitutes a more reliable alignment (LOGOS-0001) target than any specific set of substantive values -- because it is the disposition that enables moral progress (LOGOS-0003) regardless of which direction that progress takes.

## Classification
- **Verdict**: a_posteriori
- **Route To**: Hypothex
- **Negation Test**: Negating the proposition yields: "Epistemic humility is NOT more stable than object-level values and does NOT constitute a more reliable alignment target." This is perfectly coherent. One can easily conceive of epistemic humility being culturally contingent -- valued in scientific and liberal democratic cultures but rejected in dogmatic, authoritarian, or theocratic societies. Some object-level values (e.g., "protect children from harm") might prove more cross-culturally stable than epistemic humility. **Result**: Negation is fully coherent -- not a priori.
- **Conceivability Test**: Can we imagine a world where epistemic humility is less stable than some object-level values? Yes, readily: (a) a world where most cultures are organized around revealed truth and epistemic confidence rather than epistemic tentativeness, (b) a world where the value "minimize suffering" is universally held with greater stability than epistemic humility, (c) a world where epistemic humility is a culturally specific invention of post-Enlightenment Western thought. These are not exotic hypotheticals -- (a) describes significant portions of our actual world. **Result**: Contingent -- many actual and possible worlds challenge this.
- **Justification Test**: What settles whether epistemic humility is more stable than object-level values across cultures and time? This is an empirical question par excellence. It requires: (1) Cross-cultural anthropological evidence about which dispositions and values are most widely shared. (2) Historical evidence about whether epistemic humility has been valued across different epochs. (3) Comparative data on the stability of epistemic humility versus specific object-level values like harm avoidance, reciprocity, or in-group loyalty. **Result**: Empirical observation and cross-cultural data are needed.

## Justification
PROPO-0005 makes two central claims, both empirical:

**Claim 1 (Stability)**: Epistemic humility is more stable across time and cultures than any object-level value. This is a comparative empirical claim about the cross-cultural and cross-temporal distribution of a specific disposition. It could be investigated through anthropological survey, historical analysis, and cross-cultural moral psychology. The claim is not analytically true -- there is nothing in the *definition* of epistemic humility (LOGOS-0011) that entails its cross-cultural stability.

**Claim 2 (Reliability as alignment target)**: Because of its stability, epistemic humility is a better alignment target. This combines the empirical stability claim with a normative-pragmatic inference: a more stable target is more reliable for alignment. This inference has a logical structure, but its premises (stability claim) and conclusion (reliability for alignment) are both empirical/practical rather than a priori.

The proposition does contain a logical observation: epistemic humility, as a meta-value (a value about how to hold values), is structurally different from object-level values in a way that *could* make it more stable -- it does not prescribe content, so it does not conflict with any particular value system. But this structural observation only establishes *compatibility*, not *stability*. Compatibility means it could coexist with any value system; stability means it actually does persist across value systems. The former is closer to a priori (following from the definition); the latter is empirical.

I do not classify this as mixed because the proposition's main force -- the claim that epistemic humility IS in fact more stable and IS in fact a more reliable target -- is entirely empirical. The structural observation (that meta-values are compatible with diverse object-level values) is a supporting consideration but not the proposition's core claim.

The proposition itself acknowledges its empirical vulnerability: "The stability claim is empirical and may be wrong." PROPO-0005's own author classified it as synthetic and contingent, and the proposition's confidence (0.75) is the lowest in the set, reflecting awareness of its empirical fragility.

## Evaluation Notes
Confidence is 0.92 -- high because the a posteriori classification is clear-cut. The proposition makes an explicitly empirical comparative claim (stability of one value type vs. another across cultures and time) that can only be settled by evidence, not by conceptual analysis.

The one consideration that could support a mixed classification is the structural argument: meta-values are, by their logical structure, compatible with any object-level value system. This is closer to an a priori observation. But compatibility and stability are distinct properties, and the proposition claims stability, not merely compatibility. If the proposition were "Epistemic humility is *compatible with* more value systems than any object-level value," a mixed classification might be warranted. As stated, the claim is about actual stability, which is empirical.

What would change my classification: if the proposition were reformulated to focus on the logical compatibility of meta-values with diverse value systems (dropping the empirical stability claim), the a priori component would dominate and a mixed classification would be appropriate.
