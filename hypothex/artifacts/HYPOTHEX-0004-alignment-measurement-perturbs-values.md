# Hypothesis: Value Measurement Systematically Distorts Values in Alignment-Relevant Ways

## Statement
PROPO-0004 (The Alignment Heisenberg Proposition) claims that any attempt to measure, formalize, or specify human values for alignment purposes changes those values, producing a systematic gap between values-as-lived and values-as-measured. ORI-0004 classified this as a posteriori because the perturbation claim is empirical, not logically necessary. This hypothesis structures the empirical content into a testable prediction with falsification criteria.

## Hypothesis Structure
- **Claim**: When human values are elicited for the purpose of alignment (through self-report, revealed preference analysis, behavioral observation, or any formalization process), the elicitation process produces a systematic and directional distortion -- not merely random noise -- such that the resulting value profile diverges from the agent's operative values in predictable ways: toward greater internal consistency, toward social desirability, toward articulable principles at the expense of tacit dispositions, and toward values that are easy to specify at the expense of those that resist formalization.
- **If-Then Form**: If value measurement systematically perturbs values in alignment-relevant ways, then: (1) alignment systems trained on stated preferences (e.g., RLHF) should produce systematically different behavioral outputs from alignment systems trained on revealed preferences (behavioral data), and both should diverge from human judgments in genuinely novel situations not represented in the training data; (2) the divergence should be directional (not random), with stated-preference systems skewing toward socially desirable, internally consistent, and easily articulated values; and (3) the magnitude of divergence should increase with the degree to which the measurement process makes the subject aware that their values are being captured for alignment purposes.
- **Null Hypothesis**: Value measurement introduces noise but not systematic distortion. The gap between values-as-lived and values-as-measured is attributable to measurement imprecision, and improvements in elicitation methodology will cause the gap to narrow monotonically. Stated preferences and revealed preferences converge as elicitation methods improve, and awareness of the alignment purpose does not systematically shift the value profile in any direction.
- **Falsification Criteria**: The hypothesis would be falsified by: (1) demonstrating that stated-preference-trained and revealed-preference-trained alignment systems converge in their behavioral outputs as elicitation quality improves, rather than maintaining or widening a systematic gap; (2) demonstrating that the gap between stated and revealed preferences in value-laden contexts is non-directional (random noise rather than systematic skew); (3) demonstrating that subjects who are explicitly told their values will be used for AI alignment provide value profiles statistically indistinguishable from those of subjects who believe they are participating in an unrelated study; or (4) constructing a value measurement process that provably does not alter the subject's subsequent value-guided behavior.

## Evidence Framework
- **Confirming Evidence**: (a) Experimental studies showing that individuals who articulate their values subsequently make different choices from a control group that was not asked to articulate, and the difference is directional (toward the articulated values and away from tacit preferences). (b) Documented cases of Goodhart's Law in alignment-adjacent domains: RLHF systems that optimize for human approval ratings producing outputs that score well on ratings but are judged as less genuinely helpful when evaluated through behavioral proxies. (c) Cross-method divergence studies: the same individuals providing materially different value profiles when measured via self-report, conjoint analysis, behavioral observation, and neuroimaging, with the direction of divergence tracking the degree of reflective articulation required. (d) Evidence from social psychology that awareness of observation changes moral behavior in predictable directions (the "watching eyes" effect, the Hawthorne effect applied to moral judgment). (e) Historical evidence that the formalization of values in legal codes or organizational mission statements changes the enacted values of the institutions adopting them (specification-as-intervention, not specification-as-measurement).
- **Disconfirming Evidence**: (a) Convergence between stated and revealed preferences as elicitation methods improve (e.g., informed preference methods, extended deliberation protocols). (b) Evidence that value perturbation effects are small relative to inter-individual variation, making them practically negligible for alignment. (c) Successful construction of value profiles that predict behavior in genuinely novel situations with accuracy comparable to predictions in familiar situations. (d) Evidence that the observer effect in values is transient -- values return to their pre-measurement state after the measurement context ends, making the perturbation practically irrelevant for long-run alignment.
- **Crucial Experiment**: Recruit 600 participants. Randomly assign to three groups of 200. Group A provides value profiles through explicit self-report with full awareness that the profiles will be used for AI alignment. Group B provides value profiles through the same self-report instrument but is told it is a personality study. Group C is not asked for value profiles at all -- their values are inferred purely from behavioral observation in a series of moral dilemmas. Six months later, all 600 participants encounter 20 novel moral scenarios. Measure the predictive accuracy of each group's value profiles (for A and B, the explicit profiles; for C, the behavioral profiles) against actual choices. If Group C's behavioral profiles outperform both explicit groups, and Group A's profiles underperform Group B's (showing that alignment-purpose awareness adds distortion beyond the general measurement effect), the hypothesis is strongly supported. If all three groups converge, the hypothesis fails.
- **Prior Probability**: 0.70. The existing evidence is substantial but domain-transferred: Goodhart's Law, the Hawthorne effect, preference construction theory (Slovic, 1995), and the stated/revealed preference gap in economics all point toward measurement perturbation. However, the specific claim that this perturbation is alignment-relevant (large enough and directional enough to meaningfully corrupt an alignment system) has not been directly tested. The prior is above 0.5 because multiple independent lines of evidence converge, but below 0.8 because the magnitude and practical significance of the perturbation in the specific alignment context remain uncertain.

## Alternative Hypotheses
1. **Measurement-as-Construction Hypothesis**: Values are not pre-existing entities that get perturbed by measurement; they are actively constructed during the elicitation process. What appears to be "perturbation" is actually the creation of values that did not previously exist in determinate form. Under this alternative, the alignment problem is not that measurement distorts values but that values are ontologically indeterminate until measured -- a more radical claim that dissolves the distinction between values-as-lived and values-as-measured by denying that "values-as-lived" is a well-defined category. This alternative is consistent with AXIOM-0009 (epistemic opacity) but reinterprets it: values are not hidden behind opacity but genuinely do not exist in a determinate state until elicited.
2. **Asymmetric Perturbation Hypothesis**: Measurement perturbs some values but not others. Specifically, values that are deeply embodied and habitual (e.g., harm aversion, reciprocity) are robust to measurement, while values that are more cognitive and reflective (e.g., fairness principles, environmental ethics) are highly susceptible to measurement perturbation. Under this alternative, a tiered alignment strategy is possible: align directly to robust values, and use process-based methods only for fragile values.
3. **Adaptation Hypothesis**: Value measurement does perturb values initially, but the perturbation is self-correcting. When humans experience the consequences of acting on their measured-and-specified values, they notice the misalignment between the specification and their lived experience, and they update. Under this alternative, measurement perturbation is a real but solvable problem: iterative measurement with feedback loops converges toward the genuine values, not away from them.

## Thought Experiments

**Thought Experiment 1 -- The Transparent Journal**: Imagine a person who keeps a perfectly detailed diary of every moral decision, every felt value, every moment of ethical conflict. Now imagine this diary is used to build an alignment profile. The diary itself is a measurement instrument -- the act of journaling forces the person to articulate values that were previously tacit. Does the journalist's value profile, built from years of careful self-observation, converge toward their "true" values, or does the practice of journaling itself reshape the values it records? If the latter, then even the most naturalistic, low-intervention measurement process perturbs its target. This thought experiment probes the boundary between observation and intervention in the value domain.

**Thought Experiment 2 -- The Two Alignment Labs**: Two AI alignment labs independently develop alignment systems for the same population. Lab A uses RLHF with human raters who know their judgments shape the AI's behavior. Lab B uses purely observational methods -- analyzing behavioral data the population generates without awareness of the alignment project. If the hypothesis is correct, Lab A and Lab B produce measurably different AI systems, with Lab A's system skewing toward socially desirable and explicitly articulable values. If Lab A's and Lab B's systems are behaviorally equivalent, the hypothesis is weakened.

**Thought Experiment 3 -- The Value Heisenberg Limit**: Is there a fundamental lower bound on measurement perturbation in the value domain, analogous to the Heisenberg uncertainty principle in physics? The physics case is clear: the limit arises from the mathematical structure of quantum mechanics (non-commuting observables). In the value domain, the proposed limit would arise from the structure of reflexive agency: any measurement that requires the agent to represent its own values engages the same cognitive processes that produce and revise values, making measurement and modification inseparable. But this argument is not as clean as the physics case -- it is possible that some values (deeply automatic, pre-reflective ones) can be measured without engaging the reflective apparatus. The thought experiment suggests that the perturbation limit may be value-type-dependent rather than universal.

The primary practical challenge is the six-month longitudinal component and the need for genuinely novel moral scenarios that none of the participants have previously encountered.

## Evaluation Notes
This hypothesis occupies an interesting epistemic position: it is testing an axiom (AXIOM-0010) in its specific application to the alignment domain. If the hypothesis is confirmed, AXIOM-0010 is empirically supported in the most relevant context. If falsified, AXIOM-0010 may still hold in general but would need qualification: the alignment measurement problem would be less severe than the axiom suggests, making iterative specification refinement a more viable alignment strategy than the framework currently assumes.

The most significant alternative is the Measurement-as-Construction hypothesis, because it challenges the ontological presupposition of the Heisenberg framing: that there exists a determinate pre-measurement state that measurement distorts. If values are genuinely indeterminate prior to elicitation, the entire measurement-perturbation framing is misconceived, and the alignment problem needs to be reframed around co-construction rather than measurement accuracy.
