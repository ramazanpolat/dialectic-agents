# Hypothesis: The Majority of Real-World Value Conflicts Are Dialectically Resolvable Rather Than Tragic

## Statement
PROPO-0010 (The Convergence Tension Proposition) identifies a tension between AXIOM-0007 (dialectical convergence) and AXIOM-0011 (irreducible value pluralism): the viability of dialectical alignment depends on the empirical ratio of resolvable ("dialectical") to irreconcilable ("tragic") value conflicts. ORI-0010 classified this as mixed, with the a priori component being the identification of the tension (routed to Tribunal) and the a posteriori component being the empirical question about this ratio. This hypothesis structures the empirical component: what is the actual ratio of dialectical to tragic value conflicts in the domain relevant to alignment?

## Hypothesis Structure
- **Claim**: In the domain of value conflicts relevant to AI alignment, the majority (more than 50%) of conflicts that initially appear irreconcilable are in fact dialectically resolvable -- meaning that a higher-order synthesis can be constructed that preserves the core concerns of both sides, even if it does not fully satisfy either side's maximalist position. The fraction of genuinely tragic conflicts (where any resolution involves genuine, uncompensated loss of something valuable) is a minority, and these tragic conflicts cluster in specific domains (existential identity conflicts, sacred value violations, zero-sum resource allocation under extreme scarcity) rather than being evenly distributed across the value landscape.
- **If-Then Form**: If the majority of value conflicts are dialectically resolvable, then: (1) structured dialectical processes (thesis-antithesis-synthesis deliberation formats) applied to a representative sample of value conflicts should produce syntheses that are rated as acceptable by supermajorities of participants holding initially opposed positions in more than 50% of cases; (2) the unresolvable cases should cluster in identifiable domains rather than appearing randomly across the value landscape; (3) the proportion of resolvable conflicts should increase with the quality and duration of the dialectical process (more time and better facilitation should resolve more conflicts, up to a ceiling set by the genuinely tragic ones); and (4) syntheses produced for resolvable conflicts should prove durable -- re-evaluated at a later time, participants should continue to endorse them, rather than reverting to their original positions.
- **Null Hypothesis**: The majority of real-world value conflicts relevant to alignment are tragic (genuinely irreconcilable), not dialectical. What dialectical processes produce is not genuine synthesis but compromise, accommodation, or exhaustion -- participants agree not because they have reached a higher-order understanding but because they are tired of fighting or have accepted a pragmatic bargain. The apparent "resolution" of value conflicts through deliberation reflects power dynamics (who controls the process), cognitive fatigue (who gives in first), or preference falsification (who pretends to agree), not genuine dialectical synthesis. The ratio of tragic to dialectical conflicts is at least 1:1, and may be higher.
- **Falsification Criteria**: The hypothesis would be falsified by: (1) a large-scale empirical study showing that structured dialectical processes produce outcomes rated as genuinely acceptable (not merely tolerable) by supermajorities in fewer than 50% of value conflict cases from a representative sample; (2) evidence that the "resolved" cases are concentrated in easy conflicts (where initial disagreement was superficial) while all substantive conflicts remain tragic; (3) evidence that apparent dialectical resolutions are unstable -- participants who agreed to a synthesis in the deliberation context revert to their original positions when surveyed independently later; or (4) evidence that the proportion of resolvable conflicts does not increase with process quality (better facilitation and more time do not resolve more conflicts), suggesting that the unresolvable fraction is not a matter of process quality but of structural irreconcilability.

## Evidence Framework
- **Confirming Evidence**: (a) Evidence from deliberative democracy experiments: Citizens' Assemblies on contested topics (abortion in Ireland, electoral reform in British Columbia, climate policy in France) have produced supermajority agreements on issues that seemed intractable in standard political debate. The Irish Citizens' Assembly on abortion produced recommendations that subsequently won a national referendum, suggesting genuine rather than forced synthesis. (b) Evidence from the history of ideas: many value conflicts that appeared tragic in their era were eventually resolved through conceptual innovation -- the conflict between "individual liberty" and "public health" was partially resolved through the concept of "harm to others" (Mill's harm principle), which is a dialectical synthesis. (c) Evidence from negotiation research: integrative bargaining (finding win-win solutions) succeeds in a majority of conflicts that initially appear distributive (zero-sum), suggesting that most apparent irreconcilability is a failure of imagination, not a structural feature. (d) Evidence from multi-stakeholder value alignment research: when AI alignment values are negotiated through structured dialogue rather than imposed by a single group, broader coalitions of endorsement emerge.
- **Disconfirming Evidence**: (a) Evidence from intractable conflict research: some conflicts (Israeli-Palestinian, abortion rights in the US, caste systems in South Asia) have resisted resolution despite centuries of dialectical engagement, suggesting genuinely tragic structure. (b) Evidence from sacred values research (Tetlock, 2003): when values are treated as sacred (non-negotiable, non-fungible), subjects reject any synthesis that involves trading off the sacred value against any other consideration, and this rejection is resistant to deliberation. (c) Evidence that deliberative democracy successes are concentrated in relatively low-stakes, procedural questions (electoral reform, infrastructure planning) and do not extend to deep identity-linked value conflicts. (d) Evidence from moral psychology that moral dumbfounding (subjects maintain moral positions they cannot justify) persists after extensive deliberation, suggesting that some moral positions are not accessible to dialectical engagement. (e) Evidence from Berlin and Williams that the liberty-equality tension, the justice-mercy tension, and the individual-collective tension are permanent features of the moral landscape, not temporary failures of synthesis.
- **Crucial Experiment**: Construct a stratified sample of 100 value conflicts spanning five domains: (i) resource allocation (20 conflicts), (ii) rights and liberties (20), (iii) cultural and identity values (20), (iv) intergenerational obligations (20), and (v) existential and sacred values (20). For each conflict, recruit 20 participants holding genuinely opposed positions (verified through pre-screening). Subject each group to a high-quality dialectical process: 8 hours of structured deliberation over two days, with professional facilitation, balanced information provision, and a synthesis construction phase. After the deliberation, each participant privately rates whether the synthesis: (a) is acceptable (they can live with it), (b) preserves the core of their initial concern, (c) they would still endorse in one month. Apply these ratings at the one-month follow-up as well. If more than 50% of the 100 conflicts produce syntheses rated as acceptable by more than 75% of participants at the one-month follow-up, the hypothesis is supported. If fewer than 50% meet this threshold, the hypothesis is weakened. The domain stratification allows testing whether unresolvable conflicts cluster as predicted (in cultural/identity and existential/sacred domains).
- **Prior Probability**: 0.50. The prior is exactly at the point of maximum uncertainty. On one hand, deliberative democracy research provides genuine evidence that structured deliberation resolves more conflicts than expected. The history of ideas shows many "permanent" conflicts being resolved through conceptual innovation. On the other hand, the most emotionally and politically charged value conflicts (which are precisely the ones most relevant to alignment) have proven remarkably resistant to resolution. The sacred values literature provides a strong theoretical account of why some conflicts should be structurally irreconcilable. Setting the prior at 0.50 reflects genuine equipoise: I do not know whether the value landscape is primarily dialectical or primarily tragic, and this uncertainty is itself an important finding.

## Alternative Hypotheses
1. **Predominantly Tragic Hypothesis**: The majority of alignment-relevant value conflicts are genuinely tragic. Dialectical synthesis works for the easy cases but fails for the hard ones, which are precisely the cases that matter most for alignment. Apparent dialectical resolutions (like Citizens' Assemblies) succeed because they address relatively low-stakes questions where participants are willing to compromise, or because social pressure within the deliberation setting produces false consensus. The truly hard cases -- those involving sacred values, existential identity, and zero-sum existential competition -- remain permanently unresolvable.
2. **Accommodation-Not-Synthesis Hypothesis**: Most value conflicts are "resolved" not through genuine dialectical synthesis (a higher-order understanding that transcends both sides) but through accommodation (a pragmatic arrangement that both sides accept without endorsing). Democratic pluralism works not because it synthesizes values but because it manages permanent disagreement through institutions (voting, rights, courts) that do not require synthesis. Under this alternative, dialectical alignment should aim for accommodation infrastructure, not synthesis, and AXIOM-0007 should be weakened from "convergence toward truth" to "convergence toward stable coexistence."
3. **Scale-Dependent Resolution Hypothesis**: The ratio of dialectical to tragic conflicts depends on the scale of the population involved. Small, homogeneous groups can dialectically resolve a high proportion of value conflicts. Large, diverse populations encounter a higher proportion of tragic conflicts because the value diversity exceeds the capacity of any synthesis to accommodate. Under this alternative, dialectical alignment works for small-scale human-AI interactions but fails at the civilizational scale, where accommodation or heterogeneous alignment (different alignment profiles for different populations) is needed.

## Thought Experiments

**Thought Experiment 1 -- The Perfect Facilitator**: Imagine an ideal dialectical facilitator with infinite patience, perfect understanding of all participants' values, and unlimited creative capacity for constructing syntheses. This facilitator engages with a liberty-equality conflict. Can the facilitator construct a synthesis that fully preserves both liberty and equality? If not (because there are genuine cases where liberty and equality cannot both be fully satisfied -- e.g., redistributive taxation limits economic liberty to promote material equality), then this conflict is tragic even in principle, and no amount of dialectical effort can fully resolve it. If the facilitator can (perhaps by reconceptualizing liberty and equality at a higher level of abstraction), then the conflict was dialectical all along, and human failure to resolve it reflects limited creativity, not structural irreconcilability. The thought experiment tests whether the resolvability of value conflicts is a function of dialectical capacity or of the structure of the value landscape itself.

**Thought Experiment 2 -- The Alignment Constitution**: Imagine a global deliberation process tasked with producing an "alignment constitution" -- a set of values that all humans endorse for AI alignment. The process uses the best available dialectical methods. After years of deliberation, a document emerges. How many people endorse it? If a supermajority (80%+) endorses it, the value landscape is primarily dialectical, and the hypothesis is supported. If only a plurality (40-60%) endorses it, with major dissenting blocs, the value landscape is primarily tragic. If the process cannot produce even a plurality-endorsed document, the value landscape may be too fragmented for any alignment approach, not just dialectical ones. This thought experiment connects the hypothesis directly to the alignment question: the ratio of dialectical to tragic conflicts determines whether a shared alignment target can be constructed at all.

**Thought Experiment 3 -- The Thousand-Year Dialectic**: Some value conflicts have been debated for millennia (individual vs. collective, justice vs. mercy, freedom vs. security). If these are genuinely dialectical, they should be closer to resolution now than they were 2,000 years ago, because each generation of dialectical engagement should produce higher-order syntheses. Are they? The evidence is mixed: modern political philosophy has more sophisticated frameworks for these tensions than ancient philosophy, but the tensions themselves remain. This suggests one of three possibilities: (a) these are tragic conflicts that cannot be resolved (supporting the null hypothesis), (b) these are dialectical conflicts on an extremely long timescale (supporting the hypothesis but weakening its practical relevance), or (c) the dialectical capacity of human civilizations is limited, and AI-augmented dialectics could resolve conflicts that unaided human dialectics cannot (a possibility with direct alignment implications).

## Testability Rating
- **Falsifiable**: yes
- **Testable (in principle)**: yes
- **Testable (in practice)**: partially -- the crucial experiment is feasible but expensive and methodologically demanding. Constructing a representative sample of value conflicts, recruiting genuinely opposed participants, providing high-quality facilitation, and conducting longitudinal follow-up requires significant resources. The biggest methodological challenge is defining "genuine dialectical synthesis" vs. "mere compromise or exhaustion" in a way that can be operationalized through participant ratings without the researchers imposing their own understanding of what constitutes real synthesis.

## Evaluation Notes
This hypothesis has a prior of exactly 0.50, reflecting genuine equipoise -- the most honest assessment of a question where strong arguments exist on both sides. This is perhaps the most consequential hypothesis in the entire pipeline, because its answer determines whether the framework's central mechanism (dialectical synthesis) is adequate for its central task (value alignment).

If the hypothesis is confirmed (most conflicts are dialectical), the framework is on solid ground: dialectical alignment can in principle succeed, and the remaining tragic conflicts can be handled through accommodation mechanisms within a predominantly dialectical framework.

If the hypothesis is falsified (most conflicts are tragic), the framework faces a serious challenge: AXIOM-0007 must be weakened or reinterpreted for the value domain, and the alignment strategy must shift from synthesis to accommodation. This does not destroy the framework but significantly alters its character: alignment becomes a matter of managing permanent disagreement rather than converging toward shared understanding.

The Accommodation-Not-Synthesis alternative deserves special attention, because it offers a middle path that preserves the value of dialectical process without requiring convergence: the process is valuable not because it resolves conflicts but because it produces stable, legitimate arrangements for living with conflicts. This reinterpretation is consistent with a weakened AXIOM-0007 and may be the most realistic alignment strategy regardless of the hypothesis outcome.
