# Hypothesis: Human Values Change Directionally, Not Merely Randomly

## Statement
The empirical component of PROPO-0001 (The Freezing Proposition) asserts that human values change over time and that these changes constitute moral progress -- directional improvement rather than mere drift. This hypothesis structures that empirical claim for testing.

## Hypothesis Structure
- **Claim**: Human moral values, as measured by behavioral norms and institutional practices across cultures, change over time in a pattern that exhibits directional structure (expanding circles of moral concern, increasing consistency between stated principles and applied practices) rather than random walk.
- **If-Then Form**: If human values change directionally (constituting moral progress per LOGOS-0003), then successive historical epochs should show measurable expansion of the scope of moral consideration (who counts as a moral patient) and measurable reduction in the gap between stated moral principles and actual practices -- and these trends should be robust across independent civilizations.
- **Null Hypothesis**: Human values fluctuate over time without directional structure. Any apparent "progress" is an artifact of presentist bias -- each generation reinterprets history to make its own values appear as the culmination of a trend. The actual pattern is a random walk in value-space, or cyclical oscillation, with no net directionality across sufficiently long timescales.
- **Falsification Criteria**: The hypothesis would be falsified by demonstrating that: (1) the expansion of moral consideration across civilizations is not robust -- some civilizations show sustained contraction of moral circles without external disruption, or (2) the appearance of directionality is entirely an artifact of survivorship bias (we only study civilizations that happened to trend in the direction we now call "progress"), or (3) across a sufficiently large sample of independent cultural lineages, the distribution of value-change directions is indistinguishable from random.

## Evidence Framework
- **Confirming Evidence**: (a) Cross-civilizational convergence on expanding moral circles (abolition of slavery, extension of rights to previously excluded groups) arising independently in multiple cultural lineages. (b) Measurable reduction in normalized rates of interpersonal violence across centuries (Pinker's thesis). (c) Historical cases where moral regression (e.g., totalitarian regimes) proved unstable and reverted, suggesting a directional attractor. (d) Formal moral frameworks becoming more internally consistent over time (fewer ad hoc exceptions).
- **Disconfirming Evidence**: (a) Sustained moral contraction in isolated civilizations without external pressure. (b) Evidence that moral "progress" narratives are systematically constructed post hoc and do not survive prospective testing. (c) Cultures with long stable histories that show no directional change in moral norms. (d) Evidence that expanded moral concern correlates only with material prosperity, not with any intrinsic moral mechanism -- implying that economic collapse would reverse "progress" entirely.
- **Crucial Experiment**: A longitudinal, cross-cultural comparison of moral scope (who is considered a moral patient) across at least five independent civilizational lineages over at least 500 years, using a pre-registered coding scheme that does not assume the direction of progress. If the trajectories are statistically indistinguishable from random walks, the hypothesis fails. If they show correlated directionality despite cultural independence, the hypothesis gains strong support.
- **Prior Probability**: 0.60. The historical record within the Western tradition is suggestive but not conclusive. The selection bias concern is serious: we may notice "progress" because we are embedded in a tradition that valorizes it. Cross-cultural data outside the Western tradition is more ambiguous. The prior is above 0.5 because multiple independent lines of evidence converge, but below 0.7 because the random-walk alternative has not been rigorously excluded.

## Alternative Hypotheses
1. **Random Walk Hypothesis**: Value changes across civilizations are stochastic. What looks like progress from the present is pattern-matching on noise. Different starting positions and different random trajectories would produce equally convincing "progress" narratives when viewed retrospectively from any point.
2. **Material Conditions Hypothesis**: Apparent moral progress is entirely an epiphenomenon of increasing material prosperity, technological capacity, and reduced scarcity. Values do not change directionally on their own; they are downstream of economic conditions. Moral "regression" in times of scarcity (e.g., wartime atrocities) is the normal state; moral "progress" in times of abundance is the anomaly.
3. **Ratchet-with-Regression Hypothesis**: Moral progress is real but non-monotonic. There is a weak directional attractor, but it is frequently overwhelmed by local conditions (war, plague, resource collapse), producing a pattern of two-steps-forward-one-step-back that is distinguishable from random walk only over very long timescales. Under this alternative, PROPO-0001's concern about "freezing" moral progress is less urgent, because the directionality is weak enough that brief freezes would not significantly impede it.

## Thought Experiments

**Thought Experiment 1 -- The Frozen Civilization**: Imagine a civilization that achieved perfect specification-based alignment in 1800 CE, locking in the moral values of that era. By 2025, this civilization would still practice or tolerate: legal slavery, denial of women's suffrage, child labor, criminalization of homosexuality, and no concept of environmental ethics. The distance between 1800-values and 2025-values is the empirical measure of how much would be lost by freezing. If this distance is large and directional (not merely different), the hypothesis is supported.

**Thought Experiment 2 -- The Amnesiac Civilization**: Imagine a civilization that periodically loses all cultural memory of its moral traditions (say, every 200 years) and must reconstruct moral norms from scratch. If moral progress is directional, these independently reconstructed moral systems should converge on similar expansions of moral scope. If moral "progress" is merely cultural accumulation, the amnesiac civilization should wander randomly in value-space. The degree of convergence across independent reconstructions measures the strength of the directional attractor.

**Thought Experiment 3 -- The Alien Ethnographer**: An alien ethnographer with no stake in human moral narratives examines the complete record of human value-change. Would this observer identify directionality, or only the pattern-matching of a species that tells itself comforting stories? This thought experiment forces us to ask: is there a direction-detection method that does not smuggle in human-centric assumptions about what counts as "better"?

## Testability Rating
- **Falsifiable**: yes
- **Testable (in principle)**: yes
- **Testable (in practice)**: partially -- the required cross-civilizational dataset exists in fragmentary form (historical and anthropological records), but constructing a rigorous, unbiased sample is methodologically challenging. Pre-registration of what counts as "directional" vs. "random" change is essential to avoid circular reasoning.

## Evaluation Notes
This hypothesis occupies a precarious position: it is testing an empirical claim that is deeply embedded in the framework's self-understanding. If the hypothesis fails (values change randomly, not directionally), PROPO-0001 still holds in a weaker form -- specification-based alignment would still fail to track value change -- but the *normative urgency* of the Freezing Proposition diminishes. Random drift is less concerning than directional progress, because there is no "right direction" to impede.

The hypothesis is genuinely falsifiable, but the falsification requires overcoming deep methodological challenges: defining "directionality" without circularity, constructing unbiased cross-cultural samples, and distinguishing genuine directional trends from retrospective pattern-matching. These challenges are serious but not insuperable.
