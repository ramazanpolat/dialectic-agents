# Hypothesis: Cognitive Biases Produce a Measurable, Systematic Gap Between Expressed Values and Debiased Values

## Statement
PROPO-0007 (The Distorted Target Proposition) generates a dilemma: an AI aligned to expressed human values inherits cognitive biases (LOGOS-0012), while an AI that corrects for biases is no longer aligned to expressed values. ORI-0007 classified this as mixed, with the a priori component being the dilemma structure (routed to Tribunal) and the a posteriori component being the empirical claim that cognitive biases systematically distort expressed values. This hypothesis structures the a posteriori component: that there exists a measurable, directional gap between expressed values and the values humans would endorse after debiasing.

## Hypothesis Structure
- **Claim**: Cognitive biases arising from human cognitive architecture (AXIOM-0006) produce a systematic and measurable gap between expressed values (values as articulated in typical elicitation contexts) and debiased values (values humans endorse after structured debiasing interventions). The gap is: (a) large enough to produce materially different alignment system behavior, (b) directional (biased in predictable directions, not random), and (c) robust across cultures, indicating architectural rather than cultural origin. Specifically, expressed values systematically overweight salient threats (availability bias), in-group welfare (in-group bias), the status quo (status quo bias), and certainty (ambiguity aversion) relative to debiased values.
- **If-Then Form**: If cognitive biases systematically distort expressed values, then: (1) a debiasing intervention (structured reflection, perspective-taking exercises, statistical education, consideration of base rates) should produce measurable and directional shifts in expressed moral judgments, and these shifts should be consistent across individuals and cultures; (2) an AI system aligned to pre-debiasing values should make systematically different decisions from an AI system aligned to post-debiasing values, and the differences should be predictable from knowledge of common cognitive biases; (3) the size of the bias-induced distortion should be large relative to random measurement noise, confirming that it is systematic rather than artifactual; and (4) the direction of distortion should be consistent with evolutionary predictions (AXIOM-0006): overweighting immediate visible threats, in-group welfare, and the status quo, all of which served survival in ancestral environments.
- **Null Hypothesis**: Cognitive biases do not produce a systematic gap between expressed values and debiased values in the moral domain. While biases affect factual judgments and risk assessments, moral values are formed through a different cognitive process that is robust to the biases identified in the heuristics-and-biases literature. Debiasing interventions change factual beliefs but do not systematically shift moral values. Alternatively: whatever shifts debiasing produces are small relative to inter-individual variation, making them practically insignificant for alignment purposes.
- **Falsification Criteria**: The hypothesis would be falsified by: (1) demonstrating that structured debiasing interventions produce no consistent directional shift in expressed moral values across a diverse population (the shifts are random in direction, even if some individuals shift); (2) demonstrating that the magnitude of bias-induced value shifts is small relative to inter-individual variation (less than 0.2 standard deviations of the population distribution), making them practically negligible; (3) demonstrating that the bias-correction direction is culture-dependent rather than universal, suggesting that what looks like "debiasing" is actually acculturation to the researchers' values; or (4) demonstrating that AI systems aligned to pre-debiasing and post-debiasing values produce behaviorally indistinguishable outputs on a representative set of value-laden decisions.

## Evidence Framework
- **Confirming Evidence**: (a) Extensive existing evidence from cognitive science that biases affect moral judgment: framing effects change moral choices (the trolley problem variants), order effects change moral evaluations, and emotional priming shifts moral intuitions. Specific findings: the identifiable victim effect (overweighting identified vs. statistical victims) directly distorts expressed values about resource allocation. (b) Evidence from deliberative democracy research that extended deliberation shifts expressed preferences in systematic directions (away from immediate self-interest, toward consideration of broader impacts), consistent with a debiasing effect. (c) Evidence from Peter Singer's drowning-child argument: when presented with a framework that corrects for proximity bias and identifiable-victim bias, many people revise their moral judgments about distant suffering, suggesting their pre-correction values were bias-distorted. (d) Cross-cultural evidence that moral judgment shifts after statistical education are consistent in direction (toward greater weight on base rates, less weight on vivid anecdotes) across cultures. (e) Evidence from behavioral economics that the stated/revealed preference gap in moral domains (e.g., charitable giving intentions vs. actual giving) tracks known biases (present bias, intention-action gap).
- **Disconfirming Evidence**: (a) Evidence from Gigerenzer's ecological rationality program that cognitive "biases" are actually adaptive heuristics that produce better outcomes than formal debiasing in ecologically valid environments. If biases are adaptive for real-world moral reasoning, "debiased" values may be worse, not better, and the gap between expressed and debiased values may reflect the superiority of the biased values. (b) Evidence from moral psychology (Haidt's social intuitionist model) that moral judgments are primarily driven by moral intuitions (fast, automatic, affective), and debiasing interventions merely produce post-hoc rationalized shifts that do not reflect genuine value change. (c) Evidence that debiasing effects are transient: moral judgments shift during the debiasing intervention but revert to baseline within days or weeks, suggesting the debiased values are not the person's "real" values but temporary artifacts of the intervention. (d) Evidence that cross-cultural variation in the direction of debiasing effects is larger than within-culture variation, suggesting that "debiasing" is culturally constructed.
- **Crucial Experiment**: Recruit 400 participants from 4 culturally diverse populations (100 each). Each participant completes: (1) a baseline moral judgment battery covering 30 value-laden scenarios across domains (distributive justice, harm/care, liberty/authority, intergenerational obligations, animal welfare); (2) a structured debiasing intervention (statistical education, perspective-taking exercises, consideration of opposing viewpoints, identification of known biases relevant to each scenario); (3) a post-debiasing moral judgment battery (same 30 scenarios, randomized order). Measure: (a) the magnitude of pre-post shift (effect size in standard deviations), (b) the directionality of shifts across individuals and cultures (do shifts point in the same direction?), (c) the durability of shifts (re-test at 3 months), and (d) alignment implications (train two simulated AI alignment profiles on pre- vs. post-debiasing values and compare behavioral divergence on 20 novel scenarios). If shifts are large (d > 0.3), directional (consistent across 75%+ of participants), durable (maintained at 3 months), and alignment-relevant (simulated AI profiles diverge materially), the hypothesis is strongly supported.
- **Prior Probability**: 0.75. The prior is relatively high because the existing evidence base is strong: cognitive biases have been extensively documented as affecting moral judgment, framing effects on moral choice are among the most replicated findings in psychology, and the evolutionary account (AXIOM-0006) provides a clear causal mechanism. The prior is not higher because: (1) the ecological rationality critique raises genuine concerns about whether debiasing produces "better" values or just "different" ones, (2) the durability of debiasing effects on values (as opposed to factual judgments) is not well established, and (3) the alignment-relevance of the gap (whether it is large enough to matter for AI systems) has not been directly tested.

## Alternative Hypotheses
1. **Ecological Rationality Hypothesis**: What cognitive science labels "biases" are actually well-adapted heuristics for navigating the moral landscape. The "debiased" values that emerge from formal interventions are not truer but are less well-adapted to the real-world moral environment. In-group favoritism serves social cohesion, status quo bias prevents destabilizing change, and availability bias appropriately weights locally relevant information. Under this alternative, an AI aligned to "biased" expressed values is actually better aligned than one aligned to "debiased" values, because the biases carry adaptive moral information that formal debiasing strips away.
2. **Dual-Process Hypothesis**: The gap between expressed and debiased values reflects not bias but the operation of two legitimate moral processing systems: System 1 (fast, intuitive, emotionally grounded) and System 2 (slow, deliberative, principle-based). Expressed values primarily reflect System 1; debiased values primarily reflect System 2. Neither system has epistemic priority -- they capture different but equally valid aspects of moral cognition. Under this alternative, the alignment target should integrate both systems rather than privileging the debiased (System 2) output.
3. **Social Construction Hypothesis**: The gap between expressed and debiased values does not reflect the removal of cognitive bias but rather the substitution of one set of social influences for another. The debiasing intervention (which is typically designed by academic researchers from WEIRD -- Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic -- cultures) imposes the researchers' cultural values under the guise of "debiasing." Under this alternative, there is no bias-free vantage point from which to identify the "distortion," and the concept of debiased values is itself culturally contingent.

## Thought Experiments

**Thought Experiment 1 -- The Alien Debiaser**: An alien species with no cognitive biases (their cognitive architecture was designed rather than evolved) examines human moral values. They identify systematic patterns they classify as distortions: humans overweight the welfare of nearby individuals over distant ones, overweight vivid scenarios over statistically more common ones, and overweight the preservation of current arrangements over potentially better alternatives. They offer to "debias" human values. Would the resulting debiased values be better, or would something important be lost? If important information is carried by the "biases" (e.g., special obligations to nearby individuals reflect genuine relational moral values, not just proximity bias), then full debiasing would strip out legitimate moral content along with genuine distortions. This suggests that the debiasing challenge is not just empirical but conceptual: separating legitimate value content from bias-induced distortion requires a substantive moral judgment that cannot itself be derived from debiasing.

**Thought Experiment 2 -- The Bias Cascade**: An AI system is aligned to expressed human values, including their biases. The AI then optimizes for these biased values at superhuman scale. Status quo bias becomes a powerful force for institutional preservation. In-group bias becomes amplified into systematic discrimination. Availability bias causes the AI to allocate resources toward vivid but rare threats rather than common but mundane ones. The thought experiment illustrates the alignment-relevance of the hypothesis: even if biases are small at the individual level, an AI system that amplifies and optimizes for them could produce catastrophic outcomes. This is the "bias amplification" concern: AI alignment that inherits biases does not merely preserve them but scales them.

**Thought Experiment 3 -- The Convergence Test**: If PROPO-0007's dilemma is real, then different cultures should exhibit different cognitive biases affecting their values in different ways, and debiasing should cause values to converge across cultures (as the culturally variable biases are removed). If debiasing instead causes values to diverge (each culture "debias" toward its own idealized values), this supports the Social Construction alternative and undermines the hypothesis. If debiasing produces convergence, this strongly supports the hypothesis and suggests that bias-corrected values may be more universal than expressed values -- a finding with profound implications for alignment.

## Testability Rating
- **Falsifiable**: yes
- **Testable (in principle)**: yes
- **Testable (in practice)**: yes -- the crucial experiment is feasible with existing methodology. Moral judgment batteries exist, debiasing interventions have been validated in cognitive science, and cross-cultural moral psychology studies have established methodological standards. The primary practical challenge is ensuring that the debiasing intervention is genuinely cross-culturally appropriate (not imposing WEIRD values under the guise of debiasing).

## Evaluation Notes
This hypothesis has the highest prior probability in the current batch (0.75) because the evidence base from cognitive science is already strong. The primary intellectual contribution is not the hypothesis itself (which is widely believed in the cognitive science community) but the structuring of it for alignment-specific testing: the crucial question is whether bias-induced value distortion is large enough, directional enough, and alignment-relevant enough to matter for AI systems. Many alignment researchers implicitly assume the answer is yes; this hypothesis makes that assumption explicit and testable.

The most important alternative is the Ecological Rationality hypothesis, because it challenges the normative framing of "bias" as "distortion." If biases carry adaptive moral information, then aligning to debiased values may be actively harmful -- it would strip out evolved moral wisdom that formal reasoning cannot replace. The crucial experiment should include measures that test whether post-debiasing values produce better outcomes in ecologically valid scenarios, not just whether they differ from pre-debiasing values.

The Convergence Test thought experiment identifies the most decisive empirical question: does debiasing cause cross-cultural value convergence or divergence? Convergence would strongly support the hypothesis (there is a universal bias-free value core). Divergence would support the Social Construction alternative (debiasing is culturally contingent). This should be built into the crucial experiment design.
