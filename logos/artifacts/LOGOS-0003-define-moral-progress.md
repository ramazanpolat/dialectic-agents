# Definition: Moral Progress

## Statement
**Moral progress** (noun): A change in the values held by an individual or group such that the new values produce outcomes that are, by some defensible criterion, less harmful, more inclusive, or more internally consistent than the previous values.

## Justification
Moral progress is what makes the alignment problem intractable as a specification problem. If values improve over time, then aligning to current values means aiming at a target that is, by definition, not yet at its best state.

## Genus and Differentia
- **Genus**: A type of change in values over time
- **Differentia**: Distinguished from mere value drift by directionality (toward less harm, more consistency); distinguished from moral relativism by the requirement for a "defensible criterion"

## Positive Example
The abolition of slavery represents moral progress — later values (all humans deserve freedom) are more internally consistent with the stated value "all men are created equal" than the earlier values that permitted slavery.

## Negative Example
A society that shifts from valuing honesty to valuing deception has undergone value change but not moral progress — the shift cannot be defended as less harmful or more consistent.

## Evaluation Notes
The phrase "by some defensible criterion" is deliberately vague — it is itself a philosophical problem. Who decides what criterion is defensible? This circularity is part of the alignment paradox.
